Ownership means separation of properties. The city is where this separation appears most complex; understood as a densification of privately and publicly owned properties of different kinds. As boundaries these separations are sometimes difficult to detect, invisible to the naked eye. The question “who owns this land?” is most easily answered with the help of plans and legal documents. Other times there are strong indicators, in the form of walls, fences, locks, signs

and surveillance cameras.


Since ancient times demarcation has worked on two levels;

as a physical obstacle for outsiders and as a symbolic representation of power. The way in which these two layers merge and are given form can be interpreted as an expression of the property owner’s temperament; the resulting boundary can be construed as welcoming, a well-meaning warning, or a threat of violent consequences directed towards

any potential intruder.


Considered as a design question, property owners have shown both lack of imagination and ingenuity in the design of boundaries throughout history. In Högdalen in southern Stockholm – a porous 1950’s modernist

suburb with lots of green space and vague boundaries – the landlord of a group of three blocks of rental flats has chosen to combine three elements to redirect those who attempt to use a decades old shortcut between the local school and the town center. It started around 2018 when the landlord erected a four-meter-long chain-link fence between the gable and the property boundary to block the path which crosses the large yard/open-space between the blocks of flats.


Both the property on which the fence stands and the neighbouring property – like many properties in Högdalen – retain a lot of open green space around buildings. Since the owner of the neighbouring property has decided not to continue the fence on their side of the property boundary line, it can be relatively easily rounded via the neighbouring property. The abrupt end of the fence in a way illustrates the meeting between two paradigms, ubiquitous throughout the history of urban planning; urban space as closedness and openness, respectively.


The conflict between these two paradigms is made evident not only by the path and the fence, but also by deliberate damage to the fence. It was not effective enough to deter those determined to pass so the landlord had the obstacle reinforced with two additional elements. An unstable pile of large stones has been dumped next to the fence meaning that those who attempt to pass face the risk of potentially serious physical injury. The landlord has also had three thorn bushes planted next to the pile of stones and the fence adding in the

risk of tearing clothes and skin for good measure.

Permeance

Unearthing the Remnants of the Zinc Industry

The 'Permeance Project' is a research initiated during Decolonizing Architecture Advanced Studies at the Kungliga Konsthogskolan led by professor Alessandro Petti. Starting from the personal urge of connecting to my Dutch family heritage and landscape I have followed the traces of the cadmium contamination in the river Dommel upstream and into the past, uncovering a chain of events which talk about colonialism and extractions. From the zinc smelting industries across the Belgium border to the Emu Foot Province in northern Queensland, permeability reveals, through the contaminations of river biota and the riverbed soil, how Australian colonial history has become linked to the pollution of the river Dommel.


Collaborative Mapping

Situated between a scientific backdrop and artistic production, this research materializes through the medium of soil chromatographies, utilized as a form of sustainable, cameraless slow photography, which gives agency to contaminated earths by revealing their


chemical compositions. Through sampling along crucial points of the river, the soils used to create the chromatography actually unearth a mapping of pollutants. The result is a fundamental critique to both photography and cartography as anthropocentric and modern readings of the world. Moreover, through an alternate mapp ing, I wish to highlight the innate problematic of cartography as tools for colonization of land and people. In opposition, the proposed mapping of the river Dommel blurs the lines -through contam inations- between art and science, dismantling the modern from a western positionality and proposing a demodern alte rnative. By critically reviewing the dominating human centred practice of mapping, I searched for a new balance between my presence and the agency to the voices of living and non-living bodies. Through collaborat ive chromatographies, I conduct

-bring together- the voices of riverbed soil samples, permeating into each other, to speak about contam inations and map the voice of the river.

The murder of Francesco Lorusso

Bologna, 11 March 1977

At 11.30 am, a police Mobile Unit and a Carabinieri unit (Italian gendarmerie) intervened in a scuffle between a few Lotta Continua militants (an autonomous left-wing organisation) and hundreds of Communion & Liberation (a far-right militant Catholic group) at Via Irnerio 48, in the University district of Bologna —Italy.

The over-proportional use of force against the comrades

caused an escalation of violence.

Groups of students and Lotta Continua militants rushed in support of the comrades while the militaries forced them to retreat at Porta S.Donato using tear gases.

While the Mobile Unit was withdrawing along Via Irnerio to Via Centotrecento and Via Borgo di S.Pietro, Carabinieri covered them pushing back students’ attacks from Via Belle Arti and other lateral streets.

During these riots, at the crossroad between Via Irnerio and Via Bertoloni, the Carabiniere Massimo Tramontani responded at stones thrown and Molotov cocktail, shooting twelve shots with a Winchester rifle, pointing at man’s height, apparently without hitting anybody. A police brigadier witnessed the fact, and bullet cases were found on the spot.

At 1 pm circa, continuing the withdrawal, at the Via Irnerio and Via Mascarella intersection, the Carabinieri unit intercepted a group of protesters. To push them back again, Tramontani shot a series of shots at man’s height with a gun Beretta calibre 9, toward Via Mascarella, lethally hit- ting Francesco Lorusso —a Lotta Continua militant and medicine student— while he was retreating.

Lorusso continued running along Via Mascarella before falling bleeding on the ground tens meters ahead, in front of the civic 37.

During the trial, Tramontani said that he was pointing the gun at the portico’s ceiling, while witnesses at the Zanichelli —a publishing house with offices that face on that specific crossroad— saw a military leaning at a parked car —presumably to have more stability— precisely aiming at the escaping protesters.

The jury cleared Tramontani justifying the homicide as an

act of self-defence with the use of reasonable force.

During the night of March 12th, Minister of the Interior Francesco Cossiga sent armoured vehicles to crush the revolt —with the silent consent of the Italian Communist Party. Prime Minister Andreotti —leader of the Christian Democracy party— said, on TV, that this killing was “normal and inevitable”.
